# World

## Setting

Primarily London. The financial district, Mayfair, Shoreditch, and the kinds of members-only restaurants that don't have signs. Some scenes in the Home Counties — the country estates where the wealthy retreat. Possibly a sequence abroad (Davos, a private island, somewhere that concentrates wealth geographically).

The world should feel contemporary and real — real brands, real places, real cultural references. The near-future element is minimal: this is essentially now, with one speculative addition.

## The Meat

Black meat appears on the scene approximately six months before the novel begins. By the time we enter the story, it's an established trend among the ultra-wealthy but hasn't penetrated mainstream culture. It's expensive enough that most people have only heard of it.

The meat itself: very dark, almost purple-black. Rich, complex flavour that people describe in almost spiritual terms. It's addictive — not in a hand-wavey metaphorical sense, but physiologically. The more you eat, the more you need, and the effects intensify.

The transformation is gradual:
1. **Weeks 1-4**: Enhanced confidence, energy, libido. Reduced need for sleep. Looks like the person is simply thriving.
2. **Months 2-3**: Increased aggression, risk-taking, reduced empathy. Looks like arrogance or stimulant abuse.
3. **Months 4-6**: Paranoia, territorial behaviour, outbursts of violence. Social norms start to lose their grip.
4. **Month 6+**: Full deterioration. Superhumanly strong, fast, and durable, but increasingly feral. The person is still in there — they can still speak, still reason in bursts — but the predatory impulse dominates.

The source of the meat should remain ambiguous. There are hints — a New Zealand facility, a biotech company, connections to the organ trade — but the novel shouldn't deliver a tidy explanation. The horror is that the supply chain exists and nobody with power wants to look at it.

## Social Context

The novel's world is one where extreme wealth has already decoupled from social accountability. The black meat is an accelerant, not a cause. These people were already dangerous; now they're dangerous with enhanced physical capabilities and diminished restraint.

The class dimension is central but should emerge from story, not lecture. The people who can't afford the meat are, by default, the ones who remain human.
