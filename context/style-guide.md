# Style Guide

## Voice

The prose should be literary but propulsive. Think: if Martin Amis wrote a zombie novel, or if Don DeLillo had a sense of humour and a taste for pulp. The sentences should have precision and weight, but the narrative should move. This is not a slow literary meditation — it's a page-turner that happens to be well-written.

The default register is controlled, observational, with a dry wit that occasionally opens into genuine lyricism when the situation demands it. The comedy should emerge from the gap between how things are described and what's actually happening — the absurdity of extreme wealth, the banality of horror when it's wrapped in marketing language.

## Tone

Darkly comedic but never camp. The horror should be real. The comedy should make the horror worse, not softer. When a billionaire tears someone apart, it should be simultaneously funny (in its absurdity) and genuinely frightening (in its detail). The satire should never overwhelm the story — the book should work as a thriller first, and the social commentary should arise naturally from the situation rather than being delivered by the narrator.

Self-aware, but never winking at the reader. No meta-commentary about genre. No "as if this were a horror film" observations.

## Pacing

Slow burn in the first third — the reader should be seduced by the world before they realise what's wrong with it. The second third accelerates. The final third is relentless.

## Specifics

- British spellings throughout (colour, behaviour, realise, defence)
- Precise, concrete nouns. Name the brands, the restaurants, the neighbourhoods. Specificity is how you make satire land.
- Short paragraphs during action sequences. Let the prose contract when violence arrives.
- Dialogue should be witty but naturalistic. People interrupt each other. People deflect. Nobody delivers speeches.
- The sex scenes should be good — frank, physical, character-revealing. Not coy, not gratuitous.
- Violence should be visceral and specific, not choreographed or action-movie-ish. The body should feel real.
- Food descriptions are critical in this novel — the sensory language around the meat should be seductive early on and nauseating in retrospect.

## Banned

- No "suddenly" — ever
- No "he didn't know what to expect" or "nothing could have prepared him for"
- No explaining the satire. If the reader doesn't get it from the situation, adding narrator commentary won't help
- No "it was like something out of a film/novel/nightmare"
- No characters who exist only to deliver exposition
- No tidy moral lessons at the end
- No self-referential observation constructions: "he registered this the way he registered", "he noted the noting", "he watched himself watch", "he filed this the way he filed", "which was a thing he had learned to", "a specific quality of X that he had learned to Y". The narrator observes; the narrator does not observe himself observing. This is the single most important stylistic prohibition in the novel. If the character notices something, simply narrate the noticing. Do not have the character note that he is the kind of person who notices things.
