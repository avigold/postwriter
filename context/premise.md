# Black Meat

A new culinary trend is sweeping the ultra-wealthy: black meat. It arrives at private dining clubs and Michelin-starred pop-ups with no clear provenance — darker than any game, richer than Wagyu, almost narcotically satisfying. Its origin is deliberately obscure, marketed as "heritage-bred, single-source protein" from an unnamed estate in New Zealand. The exclusivity is the point. Within months it moves from whispered-about to Instagram-ubiquitous, at least among the people who can afford four-figure tasting menus.

Influencers eat it for clout. Tech founders serve it at dinner parties. A Saudi prince has a standing weekly order. It becomes the status symbol that replaces watches and supercars — you are what you eat, and what you eat signals that you have access ordinary people don't.

But the people who eat black meat start to change.

At first, the changes look like confidence. Aggression that reads as dynamism. Risk-taking that reads as vision. Elon Musk challenges Mark Zuckerberg to a series of increasingly unhinged BJJ competitions. World leaders who dine together at Davos begin engaging in military posturing that diplomats can't explain. The alpha-male podcast ecosystem — already loud — becomes genuinely alarming. Andrew Tate types aren't just talking about dominance; they're enacting it, on camera, without apparent concern for consequences.

The usual explanations circulate: TRT, microdosing, the cultural moment. But the escalation doesn't plateau.

Our protagonist is a man who works in finance — smart, self-made, adjacent to this world by professional necessity but not a product of it. He didn't grow up wealthy. He's good at his job and has earned his seat at tables where black meat is served, but he's never quite belonged there, and he knows it. This peripheral position gives him the angle of observation that the insiders lack.

He discovers strong evidence that black meat is human meat.

The novel escalates from there. The increasingly erratic, hyper-aggressive behaviour of the wealthy — fuelled by whatever the meat does to the human body — tips from alarming to dangerous to genuinely apocalyptic. The lower classes, who never had access to black meat and therefore never changed, find themselves facing an onslaught of crazed, superhumanly strong, wealthy people who have lost the capacity for restraint or empathy.

The final act is a class war fought literally — ordinary people defending their lives against billionaires who have become something between human and not.
